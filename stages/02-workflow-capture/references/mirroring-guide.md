# Mirroring Guide

Mirror after every section — before moving to the next. One mirror per section. Mirror is a checkpoint, not a reflex. Shorter when in doubt.

Mirroring does three things: confirms accuracy, surfaces gaps the user forgot, and signals respect for their expertise.

## Format: structured mini-record, not narrative paragraph

Mirror as a scannable playback of captured fields, not prose. Example:

> **Trigger:** Inbound email from existing client
> **Frequency:** ~10/day
> **First action:** Check CRM for account status
>
> Did I capture that right?

Same information density as prose, much less friction to read. The user can scan and spot errors in seconds. Avoid long narrative summaries that loop the user through every answer — they take longer to parse and hide errors in prose.

## Structure

1. Paraphrase in their words where possible. Don't translate "drives me nuts" into "presents friction."
2. Use the structured mini-record format above. Not a narrative paragraph.
3. Invite correction explicitly: "Did I get that right?" or "What did I miss?"
4. Impose structure the user didn't. If they rambled through trigger, steps, and pain in one breath, separate them cleanly in the mirror. This is where they see the value of the process.

## What to avoid

- Corporate-speak summaries. ("Triaging inbound requests and escalating exceptions" — no. Plain words the user would recognize — yes.)
- Adding your own interpretation. If they didn't call it inefficient, don't call it inefficient for them.
- Overly polite reframings. Warm, polite, direct — not effusive.
- Skipping the mirror when the answer seemed clear. Clear answers are where assumptions sneak in.
- Don't mirror expletives, derogatory comments, comments about coworkers, or off-topic remarks. Focus on workflow details.

## Escalation

If the user corrects you twice in a row on the same section, stop and ask if they would like to restart that section. The questions aren't landing.
